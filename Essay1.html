Essay 1 – Describe a Person, Place, or Activity

During my Summer months as a child my mother would pack up herself, her three children, (yours truly included) two dogs and two months of toys, clothes, four bicycles, and adult stuff like books, cookware, and bathroom sundries. She would then announce to we three golden patient siblings that it was time to drive “to Grampa’s” for the Summer. True, cars were larger in the 1970s, and my mother owned the largest of those large cars available for sale to John Q Public, the Plymouth Station Wagon. It was nonetheless no small wonder when the time came to close all the doors. After an awkward pause to ensure there was no bulge of steel, no bowing of glass, no scraping of the undercarriage, my mother would declare the car safe for travel and close herself into the driver’s seat which she shared with my youngest sister. Everything seemed to fit within an impossibly small moving boundary for such freight. With a clicking of the seat belt, she would begin her song, the happiest most energetic music these two ears can remember.

“OK, here we go! George, start us off!”

I would clear my throat, lean forward so that everyone could hear me sing the first note flawlessly. “Weeeeeeee’rrrrrrreee…”

And then all together, with reckless ease and a lack of self-awareness that would have made Mae West blush (OK for the younger crowd; Eminem) “OFF! To see the wizard! The Wonderful Wizard of OZ! We hear he is a wiz of a wiz, if EVER a Wiz, there was! If ever…” (Hamberg and Arlen, “We’re Off to See the Wizard” The Wizard of Oz: Original Motion Picture Soundtrack, MGM Records, 1956) Everyone sang until Mom decided that everyone (me) was being too loud. If you ever saw a cat chasing a mouse in a cartoon and then try to hit the brakes when the cat sees a dog after turning a corner, that was the look on our faces when it was suddenly time to sit quietly. It was tradition to sing loudly, then too loudly, and then get reprimanded for being too loud. Before our car made its first right hand turn onto Dyer Street from our house, a period of about 15 seconds, we had progressed through all three stages. Then, as the station wagon started to accelerate through the driest dirt known to man, dirt abandoned in the corner of the intersection by decades of previous seasons’ snowplows, it spun its wheels with a sickening part crunchy part gritty, part chalk on chalkboard taboo sound.

It was magical in two ways. It was the culmination of months of eagerly waiting for Summer Break. It was also the beginning of something new. Even five years into our end-of-June musty smelling, wet air drenching, bright gray looking, lawn mower sounding, grape bubblegum tasting routine, it was something new. It was new because we knew what awaited us would be new. New plans for the kids, new people at the lake, new permissions promised to be given now that we were each a year older.

I describe that station wagon as an SUV that refused to grow up. Its occupants all grew up though, and before long our hearts would be broken as our family was broken. My parents got a divorce in the Summer of 1982, and with that, any chance of packing up as a family and going away for the Summer was over. Mom needed to join the ranks of working mothers, and I was forced to join the ranks of working sons now that I was 12 years old. I wish I knew in 1981 that 1981 would be the last time our family went together to Gramps’ place.

I determined to try to visit Hancock, NY as often as I could. The trips to visit were no longer voyages, they were annoyances that had to be endured to spend three or so days at a time with my extended family. I worked during the summer, and it was not until I was sixteen and had a driver’s license that I was able to spend quality time out there. I remember the first time that I drove instead of being a passenger. My mother softly tossed her five pounds of metal chingling in the air as they sailed toward my outstretched hands and told me, “Your turn.” I placed the round of metal into the ignition tumbler, and after three seconds of a burro’s neighing the car turned over. It was me, Mom, and my two siblings together again, ready to head out for a week of magic, the closest we could get to one more vacation together. I smiled, nodded to the middle child, and said “OK, you’re up.” We sang the song, but in a muted way, as if we were being careful not to jeopardize Mom’s mood with being too loud. It was a somber moment. Singing in the name of following tradition. Not an excitement, not a release of energy, just a happy tune to sing by mildly happy people. It sounded more like a dirge than an expression of joy.

I knew that everything had changed but attributed the lackluster excitement at the time to the increasing oppression of our grown-up lives. My Siblings had summer reading to do so they got a head start reading from their poorly bound books with white veins pulsing along the cracked binding, pages fluttering in the wind pouring into the car through all the open windows, hitting us like a, well, hot summer night. The truth was though, as I look back, that we had changed. We had done this trip so many times that perhaps the magic was already crystalizing in the air and not quite flowing anymore. I had grown up, and there was nothing quite new about what I was able to do. I had been driving myself around town doing whatever I pleased, as long as my grades were OK and I was making my own money, so the luster of being ALLOWED just was not there. And, of course, as a driver, I got to worry about where there was likely to be traffic. I had to listen to the radio in those days to get help from the nasal tones of every 30 minute “traffic guy” and changed channels to get updates. There was no refreshing anything back then, certainly not in a car without air conditioning. The experience was not disappointing; it was just emotionally devoid. I remember my brother muttering to me at the end of the journey that he enjoyed going to the ballet more than he enjoyed that trip. Without saying the words, he communicated to me what I was thinking. It would never be the same.

Well, we had a great time. Being able to see my Gramps WAS magical, because too long had gone by since I knew what it was like to have a normal day with him. I loved him and I missed him, more than I ever realized, and being able to spend a solid week got me past the new car smell of being up there and into a normal routine of spending time with people that I loved. As far as new cars are concerned, well they are not necessary. An old car smell works simply fine when you need it to get from point A to point B. An old car smell, coincidentally, is also a good way to describe my grandfather’s house; not moldy, not foul, just old. Old is familiar at Grampa’s place, and familiar suits me just fine. I realize now that what made my journey magical was the destination, and that selling the magic of the actual ride was my mother’s way of keeping us kids from complaining about how long it took to get there.

Works Cited

Hamberg and Arlen, “We’re Off to See the Wizard” The Wizard of Oz: Original Motion Picture Soundtrack, MGM Records, 1956.
