Essay 2 – Empathy for a College English Student

During my freshman English class in high school, my teacher told us to write something descriptive about the school. He then walked up and down the rows of desks, looking at each of us and reciting a phrase. “The beauty”, “The tradition”, “The library”, and on and on. I am a tall person that always had to sit in the back of the class. He was running out of ideas, and as he got to me he uttered “…uh um well…” I sniffed my nose to guard against the chill of the unheated classroom. “Ah! The smell!” Proudly he turned and glided back to his desk.

He hated me. I was certain of it. Every time I turned in a writing, he would pick on me and correct every little mistake I made. The beginning of High School English was not going well. I was amazing at knowing the rules of English. I just could not adjust to using them when I needed to. I tried to justify my predicament by believing that the issue was too many things to think about. I couldn’t be interesting, conversational, and adhere to the rules of writing. It was a lie I told myself. The truth was that I just wasn’t good enough to write while adhering to all the teacher’s expectations at the same time.

I needed a win. Badly. My C grade was dangling perilously close to a D+. I stared blankly for a few minutes. “Mr. O’Shea, I need your story to be on paper, not in your head.” I looked at the clock on the wall. Fifteen minutes had gone by. I was new to this, and I was worried, two weeks into my freshman year, I would fail out of English. I desperately began writing a story about the smells coming from classrooms. I started with smelling an egg sandwich coming from the English classroom I currently occupied. It was supposed to come across as a confident, good natured jab. I wrote this.

“I walk in the room of the entrance and smell eggs on the right side and a stain on the wall to my left. Don’t know what happened but I hope it doesn’t smell like poo by the end of the day. Then I walk a little further and smell cologne at the left. No one knows what the guy is wearing because no one knows what it is. On the right the door is open so I can smell an adult diaper. Two more classes to go and then I get to smell witch hazel from the headmaster’s office. Coming up on the right…”

Then a hand comes into my view from the right and takes my paper. A trail of dark blue ink flows from my pen, held by a shaking hand that I cannot lift from the paper in time. I jolt in my chair. My paper disappears from view. My C grade was all but doomed to a life of, well, less than C. He took my paper and placed it on the top of the pile of submissions my class handed in. I swear it waved to me as I walked by it and left class. I gave my work one final glance, confidently only that the next time I saw it, great swaths of blood red ink would stain my poor work of non-fiction from edge to edge.

Tomorrow became today. Father Munzing sat at the edge of his desk staring at each student as he walked in. I was the first to enter, hoping to rush to my seat before he took notice of our class. Fat chance of that. “Stay here with me.” he said, in a calm relaxed tone. I stood next to his desk waiting for the large shirtless man with a hood over his head to walk in and chop off what was left of my ego. My teacher spoke with each student for about ten seconds, handing the previous day’s assignment back to each one. Every single student got criticism of some kind during the conversation. Every single student had a range of shameful looks, from the “I’m sorry grimace” to out-and-out despondency. Every student looked with trepidation at their corrected papers before taking a seat. When every student was seated, Father Munzing turned slightly to me and handed me my paper. “Less worrying. You are brave with the pen. You’re just sloppy. We’ll get there.”

I walked back to my seat, looking down at my graded paper. It contained the same remarks I had already seen on other kids’ assignments. I really enjoyed those few minutes standing next to his desk. I saw that no-one was coasting through the course. I wasn’t nearly as far behind as I thought I was. Maybe my teacher didn’t hate me. Maybe I should calm down and stop worrying so much about my grades. If everyone gets corrected by the teacher, and if everyone worries about how they will be graded, then why am I driving myself so crazy? It wasn’t like I had time to read my work and make corrections. My assignment was a timed exercise designed to let the teacher know where his students were in their development as writers. I would eventually learn a strategy that helped me cope with the fact that I can’t do everything at once and produce a beautiful manuscript on the first try. It is a strategy that all great writers employ. I’d like to share some thoughts from an article I read in my freshman year of college. It’s called “The Maker’s Eye, Revising Your Own Manuscripts” by Donald Murray.

A significant challenge to students is the ability to write successfully because of a mindset that when they finish writing about something, they are finished with their writing assignment. This mindset is responsible for many writing misfortunes, including the abandonment of an interesting read, a loss of perspective, and a loss of organized thought crucial to exemplary narration. “When students complete a first draft, they consider the job of writing done—and their teachers too often agree” (Murray). What separates excellent writing from pedestrian writing is the ability and willingness to review your work and make improvements to your work. Executing this process in a capable manner involves talent and dedication. One must read their own words in a detached manner, with honest and open self-criticism, utilizing a consistent process each time. “Writers must learn to be their own best enemy. They must accept the criticism of others and be suspicious of it; they must accept the praise of others and be even more suspicious of it. Writers cannot depend on others. They must detach themselves from their own pages so that they can apply both their caring and their craft to their own work.” (Murray)

The usual method is to start by finding obvious problems and fixing them, then reread and rewrite again to solve more nuanced issues with each cycle. This process should continue until the writing has accomplished both the initial goal of the author and a conformity to the aesthetic fluidity that is expected by the reader. One must keep in mind the perspective of the audience. One must babysit the genre and writing style of the piece to make sure it has not been sullied by constant rewrites. One must make sure that the information presented is still present and has not slipped from the work to improve the writing. One must also ensure that the words all make sense the way that the writer intended. One rewrite in one area could have a significant impact on another part of the story. This requires a significant investment of time and effort. There are many methods employed by different authors to help accomplish this final, acceptable creation. To succeed at creating well-balanced writing, it is not just a good idea; it is essential. “Rewriting isn't virtuous. It isn't something that ought to be done. It is simply something that most writers find they have to do to discover what they have to say and how to say it. It is a condition of the writer's life.” (Murray)

Fear itself is a fatal emotion when writing. A student must let go of their fear of bad grades before true potential can be reached. Preoccupation with a low score creates anxiety that will prevent a clear mind necessary to not only write well, but to apply the skills being taught and the absorption of the process of writing with those skills. More effort goes into doubting one’s ability to thrive than the effort to concentrate on execution. Fear manifests itself in a multitude of ways when attempting any difficult task. Writing is no exception. One might consciously or subconsciously avoid what they consider difficult material. They might try to simplify their message to avoid mistakes, and thus stifle creativity. They may fear making decisions that skirt the rules of writing, even if that choice is the best choice to express with truth and beauty. Think about any person at a job that is clearly putting their effort into keeping the job simple. It is not a very satisfactory effort. Can one expect that person to grow in their skillset if they are always avoiding the one-off situations that inevitably lead to job growth and advancement? That person might keep their job, but at what cost? Stagnation. Fear focuses the writer’s attention on the outcome rather than the necessary development leading to improvement. Such an ethic creates a self-feeding spiral leading to inferior work, which causes more fear, which causes inferior work. Releasing the fear of grading, fear of criticism, fear of self-judgement, and all the other sources of anxiety that lead to this pattern fosters attention to where it belongs; creativity, mistakes and the lessons learned, feedback. This will spiral upwards into good grades, because lessons learned lead to an increased skillset which lends itself to better writing the next time. Let go of the fear.

Open acceptance of feedback is also critical to the pursuit of good grades in the classroom. One must accept feedback without becoming defensive. The process of learning involves knowing what mistakes one is making and habitually avoiding the occurrence of said mistakes in the future. In the classroom this means being willing to hear constructive criticism and only interjecting when a statement is not understood. Pushing back regarding anything else wastes the time of the teacher and prevents the necessary absorption of what failure and success look like. The fatal attitude in accepting feedback is to shut down, become defensive, argue, or assume the “My teacher hates me” attitude. Learning opportunities are missed when the effort is spent on pushing back. It is difficult to allow oneself to be exposed as wrong. There is really no other way to accept feedback if one is unwilling to show that they are making improper choices. Acceptance of feedback leads to lessons learned and additionally leads to being more familiar with allowing one’s flaws to be exposed, which makes the next feedback session easier. Failure to own opportunities to improve leads to repeating mistakes, which stifles learning. Writing without growth will lead to inferior work, which leads to inferior grades. Gaining enlightenment in an institutional setting far outweighs the embarrassment of having to admit when flaws are present in one’s writing. It also makes it easier to grow in all areas of difficulty throughout life if one can accept feedback in the classroom. Defensiveness only hurts one’s grades and prevents true learning in the classroom, and in life.

I can relate to the difficulties students face in English. It is a common problem, trying to do well in an environment that has expectations regarding conforming to the rules of writing, and expecting submissions that are easy to read and follow along with. Just know that it can be done if one makes the necessary commitment of time and effort in reviewing their own work several times and honestly identifying what works and what does not. Doing so without fear, and without letting the ego run rampant can only help.

Works Cited

Murray, Donald M. "The Maker's Eye: Revising Your Own Manuscripts." Language Awareness: Readings for College Writers. Ed. Paul Eschholz, Alfred Rosa, and Virginia Clark. Boston: Bedford/St. Martin's, 2013. 194–98. Print. (Originally published in Writer, 1973)
