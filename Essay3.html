Essay 3 – Making Good Things Happen with Words

When this semester began, I dedicated myself to the Writing Principle that “good writing makes good things happen.” It was the idea that resonated with me most strongly because I believe that writing could brighten someone’s day, clarify a thought, educate, or convince someone to think like me. Such an accomplishment must surely make the world a better place. What I did not realize then was how much I still had to learn about writing, or how unnatural the process would feel as I tried to grow. I have discovered through my struggles in this class the relevance of two additional writing principles. “All writers have more to learn.” “Writing is not natural.” These describe my journey most appropriately of the ten principles. Through a substantial investment of time, engagement, reflection, revision, and compliance, I have become a different writer, reader, and thinker than I was at the start of English 101. I understand that writing is a lifelong learning process. I understand that managing its challenges is part of its nature, and that good writing truly can make good things happen.

Early in the semester, I confronted the uncomfortable truth behind the principle “writing is not natural.” Writing felt like a minefield of rules that I had not navigated in decades. There is adherence to grammar, structure, vocabulary. There is also an adherence to the esoteric. The flow of thought, the commitment to reader’s understanding, the need for organization and logic. Every assignment seemed like an opportunity to fail. One moment that captures this feeling occurred during an assignment requiring me to describe a person, place, or activity from two different perspectives. I spent more time choosing the topic than writing the draft because I feared choosing the “wrong” subject. Eventually, I settled on a childhood vacation home. I began writing about the ritual of packing for the summer, and before I realized it, I had written more than three hundred words and was still stuck in the driveway. The writing ran away from me. I abandoned the entire direction and started over, focusing instead on the journey to the lake. This experience showed me that writing does not unfold neatly or naturally. It requires vulnerability, flexibility, and the willingness to change course. Accepting that writing is unnatural helped me manage this truth. I write this as if I chose to accept it. In fact, the plight of writing my first essay truly gave no recourse but to accept it by the time I considered my submission complete.

As I continued through the semester, I began to understand the second principle: “all writers have more to learn.” This principle became especially clear when I reflected on my attempt to write about accents and pronunciation. I opened with a strong statement. I said that pointing out someone’s accent is “about power, expectation and belonging.” Unfortunately, I completely failed to provide the context readers needed to understand what that meant. I had tried to use brevity to sound confident and aid the reader’s feel for my logic, but instead I removed the explanation that would have made my point meaningful. I left out the nature of schools to administer discipline as being integral to the student experience, because at the time I felt that my audience, as a group of students and teachers, would already realize this function of school, making it unnecessary to dwell on. This mistake taught me that writing requires more than strong statements; it requires clarity, development, and awareness of the reader. Donald Murray reinforced this lesson in “The Maker’s Eye,” where he writes, “Good writing is essentially rewriting” (Murray). His words helped me understand that revision is not a sign of failure but a sign of growth. I began to revise more willingly, accept feedback more openly, and recognize that learning to write is a continuous process. Even experienced writers, as Murray explains, must return to their drafts repeatedly to discover what they genuinely want to say.

The course readings also helped me understand how learning changes perception. In “Two Ways of Seeing a River,” Mark Twain describes how his early experiences on the Mississippi River were filled with beauty. He described sunsets, reflections on water, and other wonders. But as he learned the technical language of the river, he began to see danger, warning signs, and shifting channels instead. He writes that “all the grace, the beauty, the poetry had gone out of the majestic river” (Twain). Twain helped me see that learning to write changes how I view my own work. What once felt overwhelming now feels navigable. What once felt like failure now feels like part of the process.

Despite all challenges, the principle that first inspired me, “good writing makes good things happen,” remains true throughout the semester. In Discussion Forum 2, I wrote that good writing makes me proud, makes others happy, and makes me think more deeply. Reading Langston Hughes’ “Harlem” strengthened this belief. Hughes offers vivid possibilities to answer the question “What happens to a dream deferred? Does it fester…? Does it stink like rotten meat…? Maybe it just sags…” (Hughes). His imagery forces the reader to use imagination to create the feelings he searches for. I realized that writing has the power to move people without grabbing the reins, so to speak, and forcing a specific flow of thought. One can simply provoke thought to create meaning. When I write with care and purpose, I can make good things happen. I do not have to win over a person. I can let their imagination produce the effect I am seeking without forcing my logic to accompany them on their reading journey.

Looking back on this semester, I can say confidently that I have achieved the first learning objective of ENG101. While there is always more to learn, my understanding of writing differs substantially from the time the course began. My journey satisfies a reasonable expectation of learning at the college level. I no longer see writing as a rigid test of perfection but as a process of learning, thinking, and discovery. I accept that writing is not natural, but I no longer fear that truth. I embrace it. I know that all writers have more to learn, and I am committed to continuing that learning. And I still believe, more strongly than ever, that good writing makes good things happen. I am ready for English 102 because I have grown not only as a writer, but as a thinker and a learner. I am better equipped to use writing to make a positive impact on others.

Works Cited

Hughes, Langston. “Harlem.” 1951. Online reference from English 101, accessed 17 April 2026.

Murray, Donald M. “The Maker’s Eye: Revising Your Own Manuscripts.” The Writer’s Handbook, edited by Caroline Shrodes et al., Holt, Rinehart, and Winston, 1982.

Twain, Mark. “Two Ways of Seeing a River.” Life on the Mississippi, James R. Osgood & Co., 1883.
