Final Reflection – Becoming a Writer

When I think about who I was as a writer at the beginning of this semester and who I am now, the difference is unmistakable. I did not walk into English 101 with confidence; I walked in with decades separated from my academic writing. I overthought every rule. I feared making mistakes that felt bigger than the assignments themselves. Through my assignments this semester I began to understand that becoming a writer is not a single moment of clarity. It is a process. It involves learning and unlearning, revising, and allowing myself to grow. It gave me experience balancing the requirements of writing etiquette with the comfort of creative writing and improving the reader’s esoteric experience.

Essay number one, the narrative about my childhood summer trips, reminded me that writing can uncover meaning I did not even know I was searching for. What began as a simple memory of packing into a station wagon became a reflection on family, change, and the way nostalgia shifts as we grow older. Writing that essay forced me to slow down, choose details intentionally, and trust that my voice could carry a story. This milestone was my first writing that helped me understand how to help the reader understand me.

Essay number two really drove home that writing is not natural. Revisiting my high‑school struggles and connecting them to Donald Murray’s ideas about revision helped me see that good writing is not about perfection on the first try. It is about being willing to rewrite, rethink, and accept feedback without fear. That essay taught me that even when writing feels messy or uncertain, the process itself is what makes improvement possible.

By the time I wrote my third essay, I had begun to see writing as a lifelong learning process. Reflecting on the Writing Principles, especially “good writing makes good things happen,” “writing is not natural,” and “all writers have more to learn”, helped me recognize how much I had changed. I no longer saw writing as a minefield of rules. I saw it as a field of opportunities to accurately show my message to all who would read it.

This semester challenged me, frustrated me, and pushed me to grow. But it also showed me that I can become a stronger writer when I let go of fear, embrace revision, and trust the process. I am leaving English 101 and moving on to English 102 armed with confidence to keep growing in an institutional environment.
